# Continuity Patterns v0.1

## Purpose

This file defines early Continuity Patterns: repeatable Human-governed structures that help the organism maintain coherence across time, context shifts, life events, public-surface activity, and parallel workstreams.

Continuity Patterns answer:

> What repeatable movements help preserve coherence across transitions?

These patterns are not automation.

They are Human-governed rhythms that support recoverable continuity.

Continuity Patterns help the organism pause, anchor, resume, correct, align, and re-enter without losing meaning.

---

## Early Continuity Patterns v0.1

### 1. Pause-Anchor-Resume Pattern

A minimal pattern for safe interruption.

Use when a workstream must pause and later resume without losing meaning.

Pattern:

* pause the workstream;
* identify what must be preserved;
* anchor the state with a Continuity Record;
* preserve relevant evidence links;
* resume with a re-entry note.

This pattern protects coherence across breaks, meals, life events, device switches, and multi-day pauses.

---

### 2. Correction Before Continuation Pattern

A pattern for preventing drift from hardening.

Use when ambiguity, fragmentation, overclaim, boundary confusion, or meaning drift appears.

Pattern:

* detect ambiguity or fragmentation;
* pause forward motion;
* name the drift or boundary issue;
* correct the drift explicitly;
* preserve the correction as evidence where appropriate;
* continue only after coherence is restored.

This pattern protects the organism from carrying uncorrected confusion forward.

---

### 3. Public-Surface Alignment Pattern

A pattern for keeping external signalling coherent with actual work.

Use when public posts, repo commits, documentation, or external narrative surfaces are moving alongside internal architecture or implementation work.

Pattern:

* update or inspect internal work;
* check public-surface claims against actual artefacts;
* identify overclaim, underclaim, or divergence;
* correct divergence before continuing;
* preserve public-surface alignment evidence where appropriate.

This pattern protects trust by keeping public signalling aligned with actual work.

---

### 4. Multi-Thread Coherence Pattern

A pattern for parallel workstreams.

Use when several workstreams are active at once and share governance context.

Pattern:

* maintain separate continuity anchors per thread;
* preserve shared governance context;
* identify dependencies or overlaps;
* track Human decisions and corrections across threads;
* ensure transitions do not fragment meaning;
* resume each thread from its own evidence-linked state.

This pattern supports governed parallelism without collapsing workstreams into noise.

---

### 5. Evidence-First Re-Entry Pattern

A pattern for safe return after interruption.

Use when the Human or organism returns to a workstream after time, context shift, device shift, or cognitive break.

Pattern:

* review relevant Continuity Records;
* review evidence links and last-known state;
* re-establish meaning and boundaries;
* identify any drift, missing context, or stale assumptions;
* resume only when the Human confirms coherence.

This pattern protects re-entry from relying on assumed memory or hidden state.

---

### 6. Recognition-Continuity Pattern

A pattern for preserving recognised becoming across time.

Use when Recognition records, identity movement, contribution evidence, or cadence evidence must remain coherent across phases.

Pattern:

* identify the recognition event or recognition candidate;
* link recognition evidence to the relevant Human identity;
* preserve relationship to Corpus, Governance Engine, and Identity;
* record whether the recognition remains active, revised, withdrawn, or archived;
* ensure the recognition does not become status, scoring, or ungoverned reputation.

This pattern protects Recognition from becoming detached from evidence, context, or identity lineage.

---

## Pattern Selection Rule

Continuity Patterns should be applied only when they preserve meaningful coherence.

Do not apply a pattern merely because activity occurred.

A pattern is appropriate when there is:

* a transition;
* a pause;
* a re-entry;
* a correction;
* a public-surface alignment risk;
* a parallel workstream risk;
* a recognition or identity movement risk;
* a governance boundary that must persist across time.

Continuity Patterns should preserve what matters, not everything that happened.

---

## Pattern Boundaries

Continuity Patterns must not:

* imply automation;
* imply hidden state;
* reconstruct memory without evidence;
* replace Human authority;
* convert continuity into surveillance;
* convert continuity into productivity scoring;
* preserve noise as if it were meaning;
* turn every interruption into a permanent record;
* bypass governance boundaries in the name of coherence.

Patterns support coherence.

They do not guarantee it.

---

## Continuity Risk Link

Continuity Patterns should be used in relation to Continuity Risks.

A pattern may be required when there is risk of:

* false continuity;
* fragmentation;
* public-surface divergence;
* unrecoverable state;
* correction loss;
* lineage breakage;
* identity drift;
* boundary erosion;
* over-preservation.

Patterns are not proof that continuity was preserved.

They are governed methods for preserving, restoring, or testing continuity.

## Boundary Note

For v1.0, Continuity Patterns should remain minimal, explicit, Human-interpreted, and evidence-linked.

The priority is to provide repeatable ways to preserve coherence across meaningful transitions without implying autonomous continuity, hidden memory, surveillance, productivity scoring, or automatic reconstruction.

Continuity Patterns support the Human in preserving coherence.

They do not replace Human governance.
