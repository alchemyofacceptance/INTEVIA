# Continuity Corrections v0.1

## Purpose

This file defines how corrections preserve coherence: how drift, ambiguity, overclaim, fragmentation, false continuity, broken lineage, or boundary confusion is identified and resolved.

Continuity Corrections answer:

> How does the organism restore coherence when something has drifted?

Correction is not failure.

Correction is governance.

Correction is how continuity remains safe.

Correction protects the organism from carrying forward drift as if it were coherence.

---

## Correction Types v0.1

Corrections may include:

* **Drift Correction** — restoring meaning after divergence.
* **Ambiguity Correction** — clarifying unclear, incomplete, or conflicting material.
* **Boundary Correction** — restoring governance limits, scope, authority, or v1.0 constraints.
* **Lineage Correction** — repairing broken arcs between commits, posts, artefacts, records, decisions, or evidence.
* **Public-Surface Correction** — aligning external signals with internal evidence.
* **Identity Correction** — resolving role, authority, responsibility, or Human-AI boundary confusion.
* **Recognition Correction** — revising Recognition so it remains evidence-linked, contextual, and non-scoring.
* **Supersession Correction** — fixing incomplete, unclear, or unsupported replacement events.
* **Archival Correction** — correcting archived lineage that was incomplete, misleading, or being mistaken for active state.
* **Re-entry Correction** — resolving stale assumptions or missing context before resuming a workstream.

Corrections must remain Human-governed.

---

## Correction Structure v0.1

A correction entry should include:

* **timestamp** — when the correction was identified, made, or ratified;
* **correction_type** — the kind of correction being recorded;
* **correction_context** — what drifted, became ambiguous, broke, overclaimed, fragmented, or lost coherence;
* **evidence_links** — records, markers, commits, posts, artefacts, screenshots, decisions, or other evidence objects;
* **continuity_markers** — relevant correction, boundary, lineage, public-surface, identity, recognition, supersession, or archival markers;
* **linked_organs** — Recognition, Corpus, Governance Engine, Identity, or other relevant domains;
* **correction_value** — what coherence the correction restores, protects, clarifies, or prevents;
* **supersession_or_archival_link** — successor object, archived object, superseded object, or related lineage where applicable;
* **human_interpretation_or_ratification** — Human-authored interpretation, correction note, decision, or ratification;
* **boundary_note** — any caution needed to prevent overclaim, hidden-state assumptions, erased lineage, or repeated drift.

Corrections must remain explicit and evidence-linked.

---

## Minimal Correction Lifecycle v0.1

A Continuity Correction may move through the following lifecycle:

* **Detected** — drift, ambiguity, overclaim, fragmentation, false continuity, or boundary issue is identified.
* **Paused** — forward motion pauses where necessary to prevent drift from hardening.
* **Interpreted** — the Human or governed process clarifies what went wrong and why it matters.
* **Corrected** — the correction is made explicitly.
* **Linked** — relevant evidence, markers, records, decisions, lineage, or public-surface artefacts are connected.
* **Ratified** — the Human confirms the correction where authority is required.
* **Resumed, Superseded, or Archived** — the affected continuity object either continues, is replaced, or is preserved as lineage.

This lifecycle is intentionally minimal for v1.0.

## Correction Distinctions

### Correction vs Supersession

Correction restores coherence in an existing continuity object or thread.

Supersession replaces one continuity object with a governed successor.

A correction may lead to supersession, but correction and supersession are not the same movement.

### Correction vs Archival

Correction restores coherence.

Archival preserves a thread, artefact, decision, or lineage when it is no longer active.

A corrected object may later be archived, but archival should not be used to hide unresolved drift.

### Correction vs Erasure

Correction does not erase history.

Correction should preserve enough lineage to show what changed, why it changed, and how coherence was restored.

The organism should not pretend drift never occurred.

### Correction vs Failure

Correction is not reputational harm.

Correction is a continuity safeguard.

A governed correction strengthens coherence because it prevents drift from becoming hidden structure.

---

## Correction Boundaries

Corrections must not:

* imply hidden state;
* imply memory reconstruction;
* erase lineage or archival context;
* convert correction into scoring or productivity;
* treat correction as reputational harm;
* preserve drift as continuity;
* override governance boundaries;
* conceal uncertainty that still matters;
* pretend no error occurred;
* replace evidence with assertion;
* allow AI-generated correction to substitute for Human ratification where authority is required.

Correction restores coherence.

It does not rewrite history.

---

## Correction Failure Modes v0.1

Correction may fail when:

* drift is misidentified or uncorrected;
* evidence is missing or unclear;
* lineage breaks remain unresolved;
* public-surface divergence persists;
* identity or recognition drift continues;
* supersession remains ambiguous;
* archival hides unresolved drift;
* Human authority is not explicit;
* the correction is made but not linked to evidence;
* the correction creates new ambiguity;
* the same drift reappears because correction context was not preserved.

Failure should trigger re-review, additional correction, supersession, archival, or Human ratification.

## Boundary Note

For v1.0, Continuity Corrections should remain explicit, minimal, Human-interpreted, Human-governed, and evidence-linked.

The priority is to restore coherence without implying hidden memory, autonomous reconstruction, behavioural scoring, erased history, or automatic authority.

Correction protects continuity by making drift visible, governed, and repairable.

Correction is how the organism learns without losing itself.
