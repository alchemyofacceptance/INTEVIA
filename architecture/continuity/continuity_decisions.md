# Continuity Decisions v0.1

## Purpose

This file defines how decisions are preserved, linked, corrected, superseded, or archived within the Continuity Engine.

Continuity Decisions answer:

> How do decisions maintain coherence across transitions, corrections, and lineage?

A Continuity Decision is not a task.

A Continuity Decision is not merely a note.

A Continuity Decision is a governed choice that shapes continuity, identity, governance, recognition, public-surface alignment, and workstream movement.

Continuity Decisions help the organism understand not only what changed, but why the change was governed.

---

## Decision Types v0.1

Continuity Decisions may include:

* **Governance Decisions** — boundaries, permissions, constraints, ratifications, or authority confirmations.
* **Correction Decisions** — drift resolution, ambiguity clarification, overclaim withdrawal, or coherence restoration.
* **Workstream Decisions** — creation, pausing, resuming, splitting, merging, superseding, or archiving threads.
* **Recognition Decisions** — confirmation, revision, withdrawal, supersession, or archival of recognition events.
* **Identity Decisions** — role, authority, responsibility, participation, or actor-boundary adjustments.
* **Supersession Decisions** — replacing an artefact, thread, decision, or version with a governed successor.
* **Public-Surface Decisions** — alignment, correction, clarification, withdrawal, or publication of external signalling.
* **Archival Decisions** — closing a thread, artefact, recognition event, or workstream into preserved lineage.

Decisions must remain Human-governed.

---

## Decision Structure v0.1

A Continuity Decision should include:

* **timestamp** — when the decision was made or ratified;
* **decision_type** — the kind of decision being recorded;
* **decision_context** — the situation, transition, correction, or governance issue that required the decision;
* **evidence_links** — commits, posts, artefacts, records, markers, corrections, screenshots, or other evidence objects;
* **continuity_markers** — relevant pause, re-entry, correction, boundary, lineage, recognition, identity, public-surface, or archival markers;
* **linked_organs** — Recognition, Corpus, Governance Engine, Identity, or other relevant domains;
* **decision_value** — what the decision preserves, changes, prevents, clarifies, or enables;
* **supersession_or_archival_link** — successor thread, archived thread, replaced artefact, or prior version where applicable;
* **human_interpretation_or_ratification** — Human-authored interpretation, correction, decision note, or ratification;
* **boundary_note** — any caution needed to prevent overclaim, false continuity, hidden-state assumptions, or governance drift.

## Minimal Decision Lifecycle v0.1

A Continuity Decision may move through the following lifecycle:

* **Proposed** — a possible decision has been identified but not yet ratified.
* **Reviewed** — the decision is examined against evidence, governance boundaries, and continuity context.
* **Ratified** — the Human confirms the decision as governed.
* **Applied** — the decision shapes a thread, artefact, marker, record, recognition event, public-surface action, or state transition.
* **Corrected** — the decision is revised because drift, ambiguity, overclaim, or new evidence requires correction.
* **Superseded** — the decision is replaced by a later governed decision.
* **Archived** — the decision is preserved as lineage but no longer actively governs current work.

This lifecycle is intentionally minimal for v1.0.

---

## Decision Layer Distinctions

A Continuity Decision is distinct from adjacent continuity objects:

* **Evidence** supports or challenges the decision.
* **Markers** signal where the decision affected continuity.
* **Records** preserve the decision context.
* **Lineage** shows how the decision shaped the path over time.
* **State Transitions** show what continuity movement the decision enabled.
* **Archival** preserves decisions that no longer actively govern.
* **Risks** identify what the decision was protecting against.

A decision should not be treated as true simply because it exists.

It becomes meaningful through evidence, Human interpretation, and governed application.

---

## Decision Boundaries

Continuity Decisions must not:

* imply hidden state;
* imply memory reconstruction;
* override governance boundaries;
* convert decisions into behavioural scoring;
* treat decisions as productivity signals;
* collapse unrelated threads into one decision path;
* preserve noise or drafts as decisions;
* claim authority without Human ratification;
* erase uncertainty, correction, supersession, or archival context;
* replace evidence with assertion;
* turn every preference or passing judgement into a continuity decision.

Decisions shape coherence.

They do not replace evidence.

---

## Decision Failure Modes v0.1

A decision may fail when:

* evidence is missing or unclear;
* drift is uncorrected;
* lineage breaks;
* public-surface alignment collapses;
* identity boundaries are unclear;
* governance context is lost;
* supersession is incomplete or ambiguous;
* the decision is applied beyond its valid scope;
* the decision is preserved without context;
* a prior decision continues to govern after being superseded;
* Human authority was not explicit.

Failure should trigger correction, re-review, supersession, archival, or Human ratification — not continuation from a false governance state.

## Boundary Note

For v1.0, Continuity Decisions should remain minimal, explicit, Human-governed, and evidence-linked.

The priority is to preserve the decisions that shape coherence without turning every activity, preference, draft, or assumption into a governed continuity decision.

Continuity Decisions preserve why continuity moved.

They do not imply hidden memory, autonomous governance, behavioural scoring, or automatic authority.
