# Continuity Risks v0.1

## Purpose

This file defines the risks associated with Continuity: how continuity can fail, be misinterpreted, or be misused, and how the organism protects itself from fragmentation, drift, false stability, hidden inference, and loss of coherence.

Continuity Risks answer:

> What threatens coherence across time, and how do we prevent it?

Continuity risk exists whenever the organism appears to remain coherent but the underlying evidence, governance, workstream state, identity linkage, or public-surface alignment no longer supports that coherence.

---

## Key Continuity Risks v0.1

Continuity risks may include:

* **False Continuity** — assuming coherence where evidence does not support it.
* **Over-preservation** — keeping noise, drafts, temporary fragments, or irrelevant artefacts.
* **Surveillance Drift** — continuity becoming behavioural tracking or hidden profiling.
* **Productivity Collapse** — continuity being mistaken for speed, output volume, or throughput.
* **Hidden State** — continuity implying memory, context, or preserved state the organism does not actually have.
* **Fragmentation** — parallel workstreams losing shared meaning or shared governance context.
* **Boundary Erosion** — continuity overriding governance constraints in the name of “keeping things moving.”
* **Identity Drift** — continuity becoming misaligned with Identity records, actor boundaries, authority, or responsibility.
* **Public-Surface Divergence** — internal work and external signalling falling out of sync.
* **Unrecoverable State** — workstreams that cannot be safely re-entered because evidence, decision context, or boundaries were not preserved.
* **Correction Loss** — corrections being made but not preserved, causing old drift to reappear later.
* **Lineage Breakage** — commits, posts, artefacts, evidence, or decisions no longer forming a coherent trail.
* **Overclaim Continuity** — presenting continuity as stronger, cleaner, or more complete than the evidence supports.

---

## Mitigation Principles v0.1

Continuity must:

* remain Human-governed;
* remain evidence-linked;
* preserve meaning, not volume;
* maintain coherence, not speed;
* support correction before drift hardens;
* maintain explicit boundaries;
* avoid hidden inference;
* avoid behavioural profiling;
* avoid productivity scoring;
* distinguish live continuity from archived lineage;
* distinguish stable evidence from temporary working state;
* maintain alignment with Recognition, Corpus, Governance Engine, and Identity.

---

## Risk Handling v0.1

When a continuity risk is detected, the organism should:

* pause before continuing the affected workstream;
* identify the specific risk type;
* locate the evidence gap, boundary issue, or coherence break;
* determine whether Human correction is required;
* preserve the correction as a continuity record where appropriate;
* avoid claiming continuity until the evidence supports it;
* resume only when the Human-governed thread is clear enough to continue safely.

Continuity risk handling is not failure.

It is how the organism protects itself from carrying forward drift.

## Boundary Note

For v1.0, Continuity Risks should remain visible, governed, and correctable.

The priority is not to eliminate all discontinuity. The priority is to detect when coherence is threatened and prevent the organism from mistaking fragmentation, drift, hidden inference, or false stability for real continuity.

Continuity must protect the organism from fragmentation without creating surveillance, false stability, productivity scoring, or ungoverned inference.

Continuity risks exist so the organism can pause, correct, and resume without losing itself.
