# Continuity Supersession v0.1

## Purpose

This file defines how supersession is governed within the Continuity Engine: how threads, artefacts, decisions, recognition events, identity movements, or public-surface signals are replaced by successors without creating false continuity.

Continuity Supersession answers:

> How does the organism replace something without losing coherence or implying hidden state?

Supersession is not deletion.

Supersession is not correction.

Supersession is not archival.

Supersession is the governed replacement of one continuity object with another.

It preserves the relationship between what came before and what now governs.

---

## Supersession Conditions v0.1

Supersession may occur when:

* a new artefact, decision, or version replaces an older one;
* a thread evolves into a successor thread with clearer scope;
* a recognition event is revised or updated;
* a governance decision is replaced by a later governed decision;
* a public-surface claim is replaced by a corrected or updated signal;
* an identity boundary or role is updated under Human authority;
* an archived lineage object needs to point to an active successor;
* a prior implementation boundary is replaced by a sharper governed boundary.

Supersession must remain Human-governed.

---

## Supersession Structure v0.1

A supersession entry should include:

* **timestamp** — when supersession occurred or was ratified;
* **superseded_object** — the prior thread, artefact, decision, recognition event, identity movement, or public-surface signal;
* **successor_object** — the governed successor now replacing it;
* **supersession_reason** — why replacement was required;
* **evidence_links** — commits, posts, artefacts, records, markers, corrections, decisions, screenshots, or other evidence objects;
* **continuity_markers** — relevant lineage, correction, boundary, public-surface, recognition, identity, or archival markers;
* **linked_organs** — Recognition, Corpus, Governance Engine, Identity, or other relevant domains;
* **supersession_value** — what coherence the supersession preserves, clarifies, or protects;
* **human_interpretation_or_ratification** — Human-authored interpretation, correction, decision note, or ratification;
* **boundary_note** — any caution needed to prevent false continuity, overclaim, hidden-state assumptions, or superseded objects continuing to govern active work.

Supersession must remain explicit and evidence-linked.

---

## Supersession Distinctions

### Supersession vs Correction

Correction resolves drift, ambiguity, overclaim, or fragmentation.

Supersession replaces one governed object with another.

A correction may lead to supersession, but correction and supersession are not the same movement.

### Supersession vs Archival

Archival preserves a thread, artefact, or decision as closed lineage.

Supersession identifies the governed successor that replaces an earlier object.

A superseded object may be archived, but archived material should not continue to govern active work unless explicitly restored.

### Supersession vs Deletion

Deletion removes or discards material.

Supersession preserves the relationship between the old object and the successor object.

Supersession should allow later review of what changed, why it changed, and what now governs.

### Supersession vs False Continuity

False continuity occurs when a superseded object continues to be treated as active without evidence or authority.

Supersession must make the replacement visible enough that the organism does not accidentally continue from an outdated state.

---

## Supersession Boundaries

Supersession must not:

* imply hidden state;
* imply memory reconstruction;
* treat the superseded object as still active;
* collapse unrelated threads into one lineage;
* preserve noise or drafts as continuity;
* override governance boundaries;
* erase correction, drift, or ambiguity that preceded the supersession;
* hide uncertainty about why replacement occurred;
* allow the superseded object to continue governing active work by default;
* present replacement as automatic rather than Human-governed.

Supersession replaces.

It does not erase history.

---

## Supersession Failure Modes v0.1

Supersession may fail when:

* the successor is unclear or unlinked;
* evidence does not support the replacement;
* lineage breaks between old and new objects;
* public-surface signals still reference the superseded object;
* identity or recognition drift occurs;
* governance context is lost;
* the superseded object continues to govern active work;
* the supersession reason is missing or ambiguous;
* archival occurs without successor linkage where successor linkage is required;
* correction history is hidden or detached from the successor.

Failure should trigger correction, re-review, archival, or Human ratification.
