# Interoperability Principle

## Constitutional Governance Doctrine

**Status:** Ratified / Active
**Authority:** Constitutional Governance Doctrine
**Index Placement:** Governance Spine → Constitutional Doctrines
**Ratification Basis:** Human ratification, 2026-06-19 — “This is the way of the HAT. 🎩”

---

INTEVIA shall never impose artificial friction to coerce users into a specific ecosystem.

The organism must remain **ecosystem-agnostic**, **connector-neutral**, and **user-sovereign** in all integrations.

INTEVIA recognises that Humans operate across multiple digital environments: Google, Microsoft, Apple, open-source systems, proprietary systems, legacy systems, and emergent platforms.

Therefore:

* External systems must be treated as first-class integration surfaces where technically and ethically feasible.
* Connectors must be designed for open-surface interoperability.
* Data flows must preserve user sovereignty, not platform sovereignty.
* Permissions must be governed by user authority, context, and consequence — not artificially restricted for ecosystem control.
* Export, import, backup, and restore pathways must remain inspectable and portable wherever possible.

INTEVIA shall not reproduce the lock-in strategies of corporate ecosystems.

It shall remain an open, governed organism — not a walled garden.

This principle is constitutional and applies to all future migrations, integrations, connector designs, permission models, import/export pathways, backup and restore flows, and architectural decisions.

---

## Governance Interpretation

This principle confirms that INTEVIA’s open-source posture is not only about transparency.

It is an anti-lock-in governance stance designed to preserve Human sovereignty across digital environments.

Open source is therefore treated as a governance posture, not merely a publication model.

---

## Scope of Application

This doctrine applies to:

* future migrations;
* ecosystem integrations;
* connector designs;
* permission models;
* import pathways;
* export pathways;
* backup flows;
* restore flows;
* data-flow governance;
* architectural decisions affecting ecosystem dependence or portability.

This doctrine may only be superseded, amended, or deprecated by explicit Human ratification through a future constitutional governance amendment.
