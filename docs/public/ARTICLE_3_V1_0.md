## 1. The Event That Revealed the Layer

A recent public AI-governance event raised familiar questions:

* what did the model do;
* who used it;
* what safeguards existed;
* whether model-level controls were enough.

Those questions matter.

But without adjudicating the specifics of any company, model, platform, or incident, the event points to a larger issue.

AI governance cannot stop at the model loop.

It also has to govern the work around the model:

* the Human decisions;
* the prompts;
* the outputs;
* the evidence;
* the lineage;
* the review steps;
* the correction paths;
* the recovery processes.

That is the missing layer.

The question is not only:

> Did the model behave?

The deeper question is:

> Can the work explain itself?

## 2. The Layer Mistake

Much of the public AI conversation moves quickly toward the most visible surfaces:

* the model;
* the prompt;
* the safety layer;
* the alignment frame;
* the platform;
* the company;
* the incident;
* the output.

Those surfaces matter.

But they are not always the decisive governance layer.

A model output can be visible while the surrounding authority movement remains unclear.

A prompt can be inspected while the work process remains unratified.

A safety layer can shape behaviour while the Human's intention, evidence trail, correction path, and lineage remain outside governance.

That is the layer mistake.

The visible output is not always the whole system of work.

## 3. The Missing Layer

Model-level safety asks whether the model behaves.

Work-layer governance asks whether the work can explain itself.

That distinction matters.

Model safety matters.

Alignment work matters.

Platform safety matters.

But they are not the whole governance problem.

The missing layer is governed Human-AI work.

Work-layer governance includes:

* Human authority;
* traceability;
* accountability;
* evidence;
* lineage;
* verification;
* recoverable process;
* claim boundaries;
* public/private surface control;
* mutation discipline.

The question is not only:

> Can the model be safer?

It is also:

> Can the work be reconstructed?

Governed work is work that can be inspected, explained, corrected, and recovered.

It makes a narrower architectural claim:

A constitution inside the AI does not, by itself, govern the system of work around it.

The relevant work-field questions include:

* Was the Human's intention preserved?
* Was the evidence trail inspectable?
* Was authority movement visible?
* Was correction possible?
* Was rollback available?
* Was lineage preserved?
* Were public claims tied to evidence?
* Did the Human remain sovereign over meaning?

If those questions are not governed, the work field remains vulnerable even when the model layer is improving.

This is not an anti-model argument.

It is a governance-layer argument.

## 5. What Work-Layer Governance Means

Work-layer governance is the discipline of making AI-assisted work inspectable, accountable, and recoverable.

It asks whether the work can show:

* who authorised it;
* what changed;
* why it changed;
* what evidence supported it;
* what constraints applied;
* what was superseded;
* what can be corrected;
* what can be recovered;
* what claim can safely be made.

This is why public AI governance needs more than output.

It needs evidence of the work.

Governance that cannot be inspected becomes trust theatre.

When a governance claim depends on inspectability, the work layer must expose enough lineage, evidence, and process for that claim to be tested.

That is the layer INTEVIA is being built to address.

## 6. HAT as the Operating Model

The Human-AI Triad, or HAT, is an operating model for governed Human-AI work.

It does not remove the Human from responsibility.

It makes Human responsibility visible.

The HAT field separates roles:

* **Human Governor** - authority, judgement, execution, accountability;
* **Vision Chamber** - meaning, doctrine, synthesis, boundary interpretation;
* **Making Engine** - implementation, mutation, repo-facing work.

In practice, governed mutation means:

1. intent is held;
2. a bounded mutation is proposed;
3. executable steps are translated;
4. the Human executes or authorises;
5. terminal or artefact evidence is returned;
6. the mutation is reviewed;
7. the repo becomes the verification surface.

The Human does not disappear from the loop.

The Human becomes the governed execution surface.
