# HPCC Level 1–3 — Assessment Surfaces v1.0

## Status: Draft for mutation

## Purpose: Define HPCC Level 1–3 Assessment identity surfaces

## Scope: Evidence interpretation, meaning protection, governance reflex, sequencing, reversibility, boundary awareness, and Level 3 restraint

## Tone: Non-evaluative, sovereignty-protecting, lineage-aware, evidence-bearing

## Claim status: Assessment identity only; not certification, ranking, transformation, admission, mastery, external validation, accreditation, deployed capability, or runtime activation

---

## 1. Purpose of the Assessment Surfaces

Assessment Surfaces define how HPCC interprets evidence.

They do not define how HPCC evaluates Humans.

Assessment in HPCC may help interpret:

* governed practice;
* meaning protection;
* sovereignty protection;
* governance reflex;
* sequencing discipline;
* reversible decision practice;
* boundary awareness;
* lineage formation;
* drift, collapse, and overclaim risks.

Assessment is evidence interpretation.

Assessment is not personal evaluation.

Assessment does not imply ranking, scoring, capability tiering, mastery, transformation, admission, certification, accreditation, or external validation.

The governing question is:

> What does the evidence allow us to say about governed practice?

Not:

> What is this Human worth?

---

## 2. Boundary Conditions

Assessment Surfaces must protect:

* Human sovereignty;
* participant meaning;
* consent boundaries;
* evidence-use boundaries;
* interpretation boundaries;
* correction rights;
* privacy where applicable;
* lineage integrity;
* reversibility where appropriate.

Assessment Surfaces must avoid:

* drift;
* collapse;
* overclaim;
* hidden scoring;
* surveillance posture;
* transformation implication;
* evaluation implication;
* certification implication;
* extraction of participant meaning.

Assessment Surfaces do not:

* assess the Human;
* score the Human;
* rank the Human;
* certify the Human;
* validate the Human;
* transform the Human;
* admit the Human;
* activate runtime;
* determine mastery;
* decide participant worth.

Assessment interprets evidence, not people.

---

## 3. Assessment Principles

### A. Meaning First

Meaning must be protected before evidence is interpreted.

If meaning is unclear, the assessment surface should say so.

No assessment statement should overwrite participant meaning.

### B. Governance Before Interpretation

Governance stabilises the field before interpretation becomes claim.

An interpretation may be useful only when the authority, evidence, consent, and lineage boundaries are clear.

### C. Evidence Before Statement

Assessment statements should be made only where evidence supports them.

The absence of evidence should not become judgement.

### D. Reversibility Where Appropriate

Assessment interpretations should remain correctable and reversible where appropriate.

Where reversibility is limited, the boundary should be named.

### E. Level Restraint

Level 1, Level 2, and Level 3 should not become status tiers.

They are increasing evidence surfaces for different kinds of governed practice.

Level 3 requires particular restraint.

Level 3 assessment identity does not activate Level 3 certification, mastery, credentialing, or external validation.

### F. Human Authority

AI may assist with evidence organisation, clustering, consistency checks, and draft synthesis.

AI may not score, rank, certify, diagnose, determine mastery, infer hidden meaning, or independently validate participants.

The Human remains the final authority.

# Level 1 Assessment Surface — v1.0

## 1. Level 1 Purpose

Level 1 assessment interprets foundational evidence from simple governed Human–AI interactions.

It may help identify whether evidence shows:

* meaning preservation;
* early drift detection;
* simple sequencing discipline;
* basic governance reflex;
* low-complexity reversible decision practice;
* foundational boundary awareness.

Level 1 assessment is not evaluation of personal worth.

Level 1 assessment is evidence interpretation.

---

## 2. Level 1 Evidence Inputs

Possible Level 1 evidence inputs include:

* Field Notes tagged for Level 1;
* Reflection Sheets tagged for Level 1;
* Meaning Preservation Records;
* Sequencing Discipline Records;
* Reversible Decision Records;
* basic boundary observations;
* participant clarification or correction requests;
* facilitator observations where consent and governance allow.

---

## 3. Level 1 Assessment Criteria

### A. Meaning Stability

Does the evidence show that the practitioner preserved intended meaning in simple AI-mediated work?

### B. Basic Drift Detection

Does the evidence show that the practitioner noticed when output, framing, or interpretation began to move away from Human intent?

### C. Foundational Sequencing Discipline

Does the evidence show that the practitioner followed a simple governed sequence before accepting, publishing, or acting on output?

### D. Basic Governance Reflex

Does the evidence show that the practitioner paused, inspected, questioned, corrected, refused, or held AI output when needed?

### E. Reversible Decisions — Low Complexity

Does the evidence show that low-complexity decisions remained reversible until enough evidence existed?

### F. Foundational Boundary Awareness

Does the evidence show awareness of basic meaning, authority, evidence, consent, or public-surface boundaries?

---

## 4. Level 1 Assessment Outputs

Possible Level 1 outputs include:

* Level 1 Assessment Record;
* Meaning Stability Statement;
* Basic Drift Detection Statement;
* Sequencing Discipline Statement;
* Basic Governance Reflex Statement;
* Reversible Decision Statement;
* Boundary Awareness Statement;
* Follow-Up Note where needed.

Default posture:

> No pass/fail. Only evidence.

# Level 2 Assessment Surface — v1.0

## 1. Level 2 Purpose

Level 2 assessment interprets intermediate evidence from governed Human–AI work under cognitive load or increased complexity.

It may help identify whether evidence shows:

* meaning correction;
* sequencing under cognitive load;
* authority movement governance;
* evidence interpretation;
* medium-complexity reversible decision practice;
* boundary enforcement.

Level 2 assessment is not performance evaluation.

Level 2 assessment is governance interpretation.

---

## 2. Level 2 Evidence Inputs

Possible Level 2 evidence inputs include:

* Field Notes tagged for Level 2;
* Reflection Sheets tagged for Level 2;
* Meaning Correction Records;
* Authority Movement Records;
* Evidence Interpretation Records;
* Reversible Decision Records;
* boundary enforcement records;
* Evaluation Event records where applicable;
* participant clarification or correction requests;
* facilitator observations where consent and governance allow.

---

## 3. Level 2 Assessment Criteria

### A. Meaning Correction

Does the evidence show that the practitioner detected and corrected meaning drift without surrendering Human intent?

### B. Sequencing Under Cognitive Load

Does the evidence show that the practitioner maintained governed sequence when work became faster, denser, or more complex?

### C. Authority Movement Governance

Does the evidence show that the practitioner identified and governed authority movement between Human, AI, artefact, evidence, facilitator, and public surface?

### D. Evidence Interpretation

Does the evidence show that the practitioner interpreted evidence without overclaim, unsupported inference, or collapse?

### E. Reversible Decisions — Medium Complexity

Does the evidence show that decisions affecting multiple artefacts, sessions, or stakeholders remained reversible where appropriate?

### F. Boundary Enforcement

Does the evidence show that the practitioner named, held, repaired, or escalated boundaries when needed?

---

## 4. Level 2 Assessment Outputs

Possible Level 2 outputs include:

* Level 2 Assessment Record;
* Meaning Correction Statement;
* Sequencing Under Load Statement;
* Authority Movement Statement;
* Evidence Interpretation Statement;
* Reversible Decision Statement;
* Boundary Enforcement Statement;
* Follow-Up Note where needed.

# Level 3 Assessment Surface — v1.0

## 1. Level 3 Purpose

Level 3 assessment interprets higher-complexity evidence from multi-layer governed fields.

Level 3 is especially sensitive.

It may help identify whether evidence shows:

* stability under multi-layer load;
* meaning resonance under acceleration;
* cognitive–meaning coupling;
* governance sequencing under acceleration;
* emergence safety;
* high-complexity reversible decision practice;
* lineage preservation under pressure.

Level 3 assessment is not certification.

Level 3 assessment is not mastery.

Level 3 assessment is lineage-sensitive evidence interpretation.

Level 3 certification, if later pursued, must remain separately authorised, Human-led, evidence-bound, and post-readiness.

---

## 2. Level 3 Evidence Inputs

Possible Level 3 evidence inputs include:

* Field Notes tagged for Level 3;
* Reflection Sheets tagged for Level 3;
* Stability Under Load Records;
* Meaning Resonance Records;
* Cognitive–Meaning Coupling Records;
* Governance Sequencing Records;
* Emergence Safety Records;
* Authority Movement Records;
* Reversible Decision Records;
* boundary integrity records;
* lineage repair records;
* controlled-emergence case notes;
* facilitator or panel observations where consent and governance allow.

Level 3 evidence should not be forced.

If the evidence is not present, the assessment surface should say so.

Absence of evidence should not become personal judgement.

---

## 3. Level 3 Assessment Criteria

### A. Stability Under Multi-Layer Load

Does the evidence show that the practitioner or field remained stable enough for Human review, correction, and continued governance?

### B. Meaning Resonance

Does the evidence show that Human meaning helped stabilise complex or accelerated work?

### C. Cognitive–Meaning Coupling

Does the evidence show useful cognitive structure forming from Human meaning without collapsing meaning into system output?

### D. Governance Sequencing

Does the evidence show decisions, evidence, correction, and ratification being sequenced under acceleration?

### E. Emergence Safety

Does the evidence show emergent patterns being recognised without being treated as proof, doctrine, authority, or mastery?

### F. Reversible Decisions — High Complexity

Does the evidence show reversibility preserved where decisions affected lineage, public surface, cohort structure, or organism-level governance?

### G. Lineage Integrity

Does the evidence show that lineage was preserved, repaired, or clarified during complex work?

## 5. Cross-Level Progression

The three assessment surfaces form a progression of evidence complexity.

* **Level 1:** foundational meaning preservation, basic drift detection, simple sequencing, low-complexity reversibility.
* **Level 2:** meaning correction, authority movement, sequencing under load, evidence interpretation, medium-complexity reversibility.
* **Level 3:** stability under multi-layer load, emergence safety, lineage-sensitive governance, high-complexity reversibility.

The levels should not be treated as a hierarchy of Humans.

They are different evidence surfaces for different complexity conditions.

A participant is not “better” because they are associated with a higher level.

The work is simply more complex, more sensitive, and more demanding of evidence.

---

## 6. Consent and Evidence-Use Boundary

Before assessment surfaces are used, participants should understand:

* what evidence may be interpreted;
* why the interpretation is being made;
* who may see the assessment record;
* whether the record may contribute to synthesis;
* whether it may be anonymised;
* whether it may be excluded;
* how correction requests work;
* how boundary enforcement works;
* whether AI may assist in organising or summarising evidence.

Consent is part of the assessment surface.

Assessment without consent boundaries risks becoming extraction.

---

AI may assist with assessment surfaces by:

* organising evidence;
* clustering themes;
* identifying repeated friction points;
* supporting lineage traceability;
* preparing draft summaries;
* flagging possible overclaim;
* identifying candidate follow-up questions.

AI may not:

* certify participants;
* diagnose participants;
* infer hidden meaning;
* determine mastery;
* decide admission;
* independently validate engagement;
* treat emergence as proof;
* replace Human review.
